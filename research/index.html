Research

We are interested in evolutionary ecology. Particular areas of research include the evolution of sexual dimorphism; sexual selection and sexual conflict; and the evolution of life history traits. We tend to focus on insects, but often include other groups. Past research has included beetles, true bugs, flies, odonates, fish, and amphibians. Approaches centre on whole organism field and lab experiments, but also include quantitative genetics, developmental genetics, gene expression analyses, comparative studies and the development of new theory.

Sexual Conflict and Antagonistic Coevolution

Sexual conflict arises when the evolutionary interests of males and females diverge, particularly over mating rate and the outcome of copulations. In water striders (Gerromorpha), males have evolved a wide variety of structures used to grasp females and coerce mating, while females have evolved counter-adaptations. This arms race generates rapid evolutionary diversification of reproductive morphology across populations and species.

We study both the proximate mechanisms underlying this coevolution (developmental genetics, gene expression) and the ecological context that determines the intensity of sexual conflict, including predation risk, population density, and environmental variation.

Evolution of Sexual Dimorphism

Sexual dimorphism—differences between males and females in morphology, physiology, behaviour, and life history—is ubiquitous in animals yet highly variable across taxa. We are interested in both the micro- and macroevolutionary processes that generate and maintain this diversity.

Using comparative approaches across insects and other animals, combined with field and laboratory experiments within species, we investigate how natural and sexual selection, as well as intralocus sexual conflict, interact to produce the extraordinary range of sex differences seen in nature.

Developmental Genetics of Trait Diversification

A key goal of our research is to link phenotypic evolution to its molecular and developmental underpinnings. In water striders, we have shown that the Hox gene Ultrabithorax controls the characteristic leg-length ratios that allow these insects to skate on water. The same gene—and other ancient developmental genes such as distalless—have been co-opted repeatedly to generate novel, sexually antagonistic traits in different lineages.

Our work combines developmental genetics, gene expression profiling, and functional assays (e.g., RNA interference) to identify the genetic changes responsible for phenotypic diversification within and between species.

Life History Evolution

Life history traits (age and size at maturity, reproductive effort, lifespan) are fundamental targets of natural selection yet show remarkable variation within and among species. We develop and test theory on how trade-offs among life history traits evolve in different ecological contexts, and how sexual conflict influences the evolution of life history strategies in each sex.

Recent collaborative work examines how the pace-of-life syndrome, epistatic gene interactions, and ecology together shape the maintenance of genetic variation in life-history genes across populations.

Model Systems

Water striders (family Gerridae and related semi-aquatic bugs, order Hemiptera) are our primary model system. They offer unique advantages: they are visually conspicuous, easy to culture, amenable to quantitative genetic and developmental approaches, and show striking interspecific variation in sexually antagonistic traits. We use both laboratory model species (e.g., Gerris incognitus) and field-collected samples from natural populations across North America.

We also work with other taxa (ambush bugs Phymata, beetles, odonates, amphibians) when they offer specific advantages for testing particular hypotheses.
