We are interested in evolutionary ecology. Particular areas of research include the evolution of sexual dimorphism; sexual selection and sexual conflict; and the evolution of life history traits. We tend to focus on insects, but often include other groups. Past research has included beetles, true bugs, flies, odonates, fish, and amphibians. Approaches centre on whole organism field and lab experiments, but also include quantitative genetics, developmental genetics, gene expression analyses, comparative studies and the development of new theory.

Locke Rowe is a Distinguished University Professor in the Department of Ecology & Evolutionary Biology at the University of Toronto. He is a Fellow of the Royal Society of Canada and the American Association for the Advancement of Science.

Research in the Rowe lab focuses on understanding the ecological and evolutionary forces that shape diversity in life history and reproductive traits, especially between the sexes. Much of this work uses water striders (Gerromorpha) as a model system to test ideas about sexual conflict, antagonistic coevolution, and the developmental genetic basis of trait diversification.

The lab employs a broad range of approaches including field and laboratory experiments, quantitative and developmental genetics, gene expression analyses, phylogenetic comparative methods, and theoretical modelling.

News & Highlights

Alignment of natural and sexual selection (2021)

Rowe and Rundle reviewed the conditions under which natural and sexual selection work in concert versus in opposition, with implications for rates of adaptation and the maintenance of genetic variation. Annual Review of Ecology, Evolution, and Systematics, 52: 499–517.

Pace-of-life and maintenance of genetic variation (2023)

New work with Arnqvist examines how ecology, pace-of-life, and epistatic selection interact to maintain genetic variation in life-history genes. Molecular Ecology, 32(17): 4713–4724.

Gerromorpha transcriptome phylogeny (2022)

A large collaborative project assembling a transcriptome-based phylogeny of the semi-aquatic bugs reveals patterns of lineage expansion across a series of new adaptive zones.
