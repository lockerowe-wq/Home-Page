About the Lab

Research in Evolutionary Ecology

A research group in the Department of Ecology & Evolutionary Biology at the University of Toronto.

We are interested in evolutionary ecology. Particular areas of research include the evolution of sexual dimorphism; sexual selection and sexual conflict; and the evolution of life history traits. We tend to focus on insects, but often include other groups. Past research has included beetles, true bugs, flies, odonates, fish, and amphibians.

Approaches centre on whole organism field and lab experiments, but also include quantitative genetics, developmental genetics, gene expression analyses, comparative studies and the development of new theory.

What We Study

Research Areas

Our work spans multiple scales — from genes to ecosystems.

Sexual Conflict & Coevolution

Studying the evolutionary arms race between males and females, from mating behaviour to reproductive morphology across populations and species.

Evolution of Sexual Dimorphism

Understanding how natural and sexual selection, and intralocus sexual conflict, produce the extraordinary diversity of sex differences seen in nature.

Developmental Genetics

Linking phenotypic evolution to molecular and developmental underpinnings — including the role of Hox genes in generating novel, sexually antagonistic traits.

Life History Evolution

Developing and testing theory on trade-offs among life history traits, and how sexual conflict shapes life history strategies in each sex.
